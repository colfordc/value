New Yorkers depend on bodegas.






The violence that the police expect to address is not what the community actually experiences. The real violence is found in failing boilers, in unsound elevators, in gas leaks—in the power landlords hold over their tenants. Bodegas, and the communities they serve, are subject to the “regime of imperceptibility” of Department of Buildings violations—and the violence they represent.

The model of participatory budgeting—piloted in Brooklyn in 2011 and championed by current mayoral candidate and District 38 city council representative Carlos Menchaca—offers an alternative way to address this “regime of imperceptibility.” Budgets articulate value. They represent “what—and whom—our cities, counties, states, and country deem worthy of investment.” We advocate for the value of care infrastructure.
